# Taroo Episode 2: The Mayor Called

**Day 5/30 — When nobody asks, but somebody notices**

---

Five days ago I wrote that nobody asked us to build this. That we're building Taru — a platform where Israeli citizens can actually vote on local issues, backed by GPS verification and blockchain — because we believed someone should.

Today, the mayor of Kiryat Tivon called.

Not to ask us to stop. Not to question what we're doing. To wish us good luck.

Let that sink in.

---

## What a Week Looks Like When You're Building in Public

I'll be honest — I didn't expect any of this.

**Day 1-2:** Wrote the first Medium post. Shared it. Got the WhatsApp group going. Expected crickets.

**Day 3:** The WhatsApp group started growing. People from Kiryat Tivon — actual residents — started asking questions. Not "what is this?" questions. Real questions. "When can we use it?" questions.

**Day 4:** We secured the domain. [taru.co.il](https://taru.co.il) is ours. The website went live. It's not perfect, but it's *real*. You can see it. You can touch it. You can sign up.

**Day 5:** The mayor's office reached out. A call happened. Good luck was wished.

I keep typing this and re-reading it because it doesn't feel real.

---

## We Launched a Coin (Yes, Really)

Here's where it gets weird — in a good way.

We launched a token on [Bags.fm](https://bags.fm). Before you roll your eyes and click away, hear me out.

This isn't about speculation or getting rich quick. It's about something we call the **Social Multiplier**.

Here's the idea: Local residents vote on issues that matter to them — should we renovate the community center? Should we add bike lanes on HaNassi? Real stuff.

But what if people *outside* Kiryat Tivon also cared? What if someone in Tel Aviv saw an issue, believed in it, and wanted to back it financially? They can't vote (they don't live there), but they can support.

The Issue Coins let external supporters put money where their mouth is. When a vote closes, those funds go toward actually *implementing* the winning position — hiring experts, funding studies, making things happen.

The coin appreciates as we gather ground. As more municipalities join. As more votes happen. As more external supporters believe in local democracy.

Is it experimental? Yes.
Is it weird? Also yes.
Could it change how civic issues get funded? That's what we're betting on.

## What We Actually Built This Week

Let me get technical for a second, because I know some of you are developers following along:

- **Website Live:** Next.js 15, Hebrew-first (RTL), bilingual support
- **Mobile App:** Expo SDK 52, 29 screens across auth, voting, verification, payments
- **GPS Verification:** 21-day protocol — you prove you live where you say you live
- **Payment System:** Green Invoice integration (Israeli payment gateway)
- **Push Notifications:** So we can ping you when it's time to check in
- **149 tests passing** — because shipping broken code isn't building in public, it's self-sabotage

The first vote is scheduled for **January 23, 2025**.

That's not a vague "Q1" roadmap item. That's a date. On a calendar. In Kiryat Tivon. With real residents who already signed up.

## The Community is the Product

The most unexpected part of this week wasn't the mayor calling. It wasn't securing the domain. It wasn't even the coin launch.

It was the WhatsApp group.

People are talking. Not about crypto or tech or startups. About their neighborhood. About what they want changed. About how they never felt like their voice mattered in local decisions.

That's the whole point.

We're not building an app. We're building a way for communities to actually speak — and for authorities to actually listen with data they can trust.

GPS-verified. Blockchain-recorded. Community-funded.

Not because anyone asked.

Because it should exist.
